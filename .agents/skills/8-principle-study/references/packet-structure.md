# Packet structure blueprint

This file is the section-by-section plan for a study packet. Each of the eight
principles becomes a concrete part of the document. Build every section listed
here, in this order.

## Contents

1. Front matter
2. Step 1 sections (Principles 1-4)
3. Step 2 sections (Principles 5-8)
4. Appendix
5. Sizing guidance by learner level

---

## 1. Front matter

**Title page.** Topic name, a subtitle such as "A Structured Study Packet", and a
line naming the method ("Built with an 8-principle learning method").

**How to use this packet.** Two short paragraphs: what the packet is, and how the
two steps work (Understanding first, Automaticity second). If the source was a
video link that could not be transcribed, state plainly here that the content is
drawn from reliable knowledge of the topic.

**The 8 principles, listed.** A short callout box naming all eight so the learner
sees the whole method at a glance.

**Table of contents.** For .docx and PDF, a real generated TOC. For HTML, a jump
menu. For Markdown, a linked list.

## 2. Step 1 — Understanding (Principles 1-4)

Open Step 1 with one short paragraph stating its goal: a correct, simple mental
model of the topic.

### Principle 1 — A map of the system

Give the learner the big picture before any detail. Two good devices:

- A **process or flow table**: the stages of how the thing works, in order, each
  with a one-line description.
- An **ecosystem or components table**: the main parts of the topic and what each
  one is.

Close with a short "Map takeaway" callout — one or two sentences naming the most
important structural fact.

### Principle 2 — Clear, simple explanations

The core teaching section. Pick the 5-9 most important ideas in the topic and
explain each under its own short sub-heading, phrased as a question the learner
would actually ask ("What is X?", "How does Y work?"). One tight paragraph each.

Rules for this section:
- Define every term the first time it appears.
- Plain language, short sentences, no unexplained jargon.
- Concrete over abstract — name real examples.

Close with an "Explanation takeaway" callout capturing the single most important
idea and the most common misconception.

### Principle 3 — Use different media

Take the *same* core ideas from Principle 2 and present them again in different
forms, because meeting an idea multiple ways helps it stick. Include at least
three of:

- **One-line summary** — the whole topic compressed into a single sentence.
- **A diagram** — a text-based flow or structure diagram in a callout box.
- **An analogy** — a vivid, accurate comparison to something familiar.
- **A comparison table** — the topic set against related things it is often
  confused with.

Close with a "Media takeaway" callout.

### Principle 4 — Short lessons

Re-package the material as 4-6 numbered micro-lessons, each one short enough to
absorb in a couple of minutes. Each lesson: a heading ("Lesson 1 — ...") and one
short paragraph. These deliberately overlap with earlier sections — that
repetition is intentional and useful.

Close with a "Short-lessons takeaway" callout.

## 3. Step 2 — Automaticity (Principles 5-8)

Open Step 2 with one short paragraph: understanding is not the same as
remembering, and these four practices make the knowledge automatic.

### Principle 5 — Test yourself (retrieval practice)

- **A quiz** of 8-12 numbered questions covering the whole topic.
- **A separate answer key** in a callout box, with model answers (not just "yes"
  — explain enough that the learner can self-mark).
- **Flashcards**: 10-15 cards, each a clear front (prompt) and back (crisp
  answer). In .docx, render each as a two-row table. In HTML, make them flip on
  click. In Markdown, use a front/back format.

Every quiz question and flashcard must be answerable from the packet's own
explanations — never test something that was not taught.

### Principle 6 — Wait to review (spaced repetition)

A review schedule, as a table, with at least five sessions at expanding gaps —
e.g. Today, Day 1, Day 3, Day 7, Day 14, Day 30. Each row: when, what to do, and
a tick box. Close with a short note on why spacing works.

### Principle 7 — Mix it up (interleaving)

A second quiz of 8-12 questions that deliberately jumps between sub-topics,
definitions, strengths, limitations, and applications, rather than going in
order. Include its own answer key. Add one sentence explaining why mixing helps:
it trains the learner to choose the right idea, not just recall a sequence.

### Principle 8 — Don't stop (overlearning)

Explain that most people stop the moment they first get something right, but
continued practice past that point is what makes recall fast and durable. Include:

- A short table of stages (first correct → comfortable → automatic) and what to
  do at each.
- A concrete overlearning plan (a few bullet actions, e.g. repeat flashcards
  until three flawless rounds, monthly cold self-test, teach it to someone).

Close with a "Final takeaway" callout that recaps both steps and the core idea:
knowledge becomes permanent through practice *after* first success.

## 4. Appendix

A quick-reference glossary table: every key term from the packet with a
one-line plain-language definition. Optionally a short "further study" note.

---

## 5. Sizing guidance by learner level

Adjust depth to the learner without changing the structure — all eight
principles always appear.

- **Complete beginner:** simplest vocabulary, more analogies, ~5 ideas in
  Principle 2, 4 lessons, 8 quiz items, 10 flashcards.
- **Student / general learner:** the default — ~7 ideas, 5 lessons, 10 quiz
  items, 12 flashcards.
- **Professional brushing up:** denser explanations, can assume some background,
  ~9 ideas, 6 lessons, 12 quiz items, 15 flashcards, more nuance in answers.

When in doubt, default to the student level.
