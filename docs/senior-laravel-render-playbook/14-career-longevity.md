# 14 — Career Longevity

> The goal isn't to be the best Laravel dev for 5 years. The goal is to still be writing software, healthy and free, in 30 years.

---

## The Long Game

A 20-year-old senior dev today retires around 2070. That's:
- 7+ major framework cycles
- 3+ language paradigm shifts
- 2-3 hardware revolutions (mobile → AI → ???)
- ~12 economic cycles
- Probably 8-15 jobs / clients

You need a career that survives all of that. This file is how.

---

## The Three Phases of a Career

### Phase 1 — Apprentice (Years 0-3)
**Goal:** Become competent. Ship things.
- Get hired anywhere that lets you code Laravel daily
- Take any project, learn from anyone
- Read source code (Laravel itself is the best textbook)
- Don't optimize for salary; optimize for learning rate
- Be paid to learn

**Mistakes to avoid:**
- Chasing $5k more for a worse codebase
- Skipping tests because nobody is checking
- Optimizing tools instead of shipping
- Quitting before you've shipped a major feature

### Phase 2 — Operator (Years 3-10)
**Goal:** Become valuable. Ship at scale.
- Take ownership of systems, not just features
- Learn business context (how the company makes money)
- Charge what you're worth ($80-150/hr freelance, $120-180k employee)
- Start a side project that could be a business
- Build a portfolio you can show

**Mistakes to avoid:**
- Job-hopping every year (looks bad after 3+ jobs)
- Going to management when you don't want to manage
- Refusing freelance "to focus" (you need cash flow optionality)
- Burning out in a high-stress role for "exposure"

### Phase 3 — Architect / Owner (Years 10+)
**Goal:** Become free. Choose work that matches life.
- Either: senior IC / staff / principal at $200-400k
- Or: agency owner ($300-500k revenue, $150-200k take)
- Or: indie / SaaS owner (varies wildly, $0-millions)
- Either way: optionality, not employment

**Mistakes to avoid:**
- Staying in a job because of golden handcuffs
- Building agency without systematized pricing/delivery
- Indie hacking without runway
- Letting tech debt accumulate (in code AND career)

## The 5 Income Streams

A resilient senior has multiple income sources:

1. **Day job / main contract** — primary, $60-200k
2. **Side consulting** — $5-30k/yr at $100-200/hr
3. **SaaS / products** — $1-50k/yr passive
4. **Investments** — index funds, real estate
5. **Skill arbitrage** — courses, books, content

You don't need all 5. But 1 alone = fragile.

## Freelance / Contract Rates

What to charge in 2026:

| Years | Hourly | Day Rate | Project Min |
|-------|--------|----------|-------------|
| 0-2 | $40-70 | $400 | $2k |
| 3-5 | $80-120 | $800 | $5k |
| 5-10 | $120-200 | $1200 | $10k |
| 10+ | $200-400 | $2000 | $20k |

Adjust ±50% for region. London/SF: +50%. SE Asia: -50%.

### Charge for Value, Not Time

A "$30k checkout rewrite" that takes 2 weeks = $1875/hr equivalent.

You charge value because:
- You know the answer in 5 minutes (years of experience)
- The implementation is just typing
- The client pays for the outcome, not the keystrokes

How to switch from hourly to value:
1. Diagnose the problem free (1-2 hour discovery)
2. Quote a fixed price tied to the outcome
3. Contract: deliverable + acceptance criteria + payment terms
4. Deliver on time (or sooner — under-promise)
5. Upsell the next outcome

### Retainer Model

Best of both:
- Monthly retainer: $3-15k/mo
- Includes: X hours, Y deliverables, response time SLA
- Predictable income for you, predictable cost for client
- Renewable yearly

Stack 3-5 retainers = $20-50k/mo stable income while you work 30-40 hours/week.

## The SaaS Side-Project Path

Step 1 (Year 1):
- Build for yourself first (scratch your own itch)
- Use your Laravel scaffold (chapter 03)
- Host on Render Starter ($40/mo)
- Launch when scary

Step 2 (Year 1-2):
- First paying customer ($10/mo)
- Iterate based on real feedback
- Charge more than you think you should
- Stop free tier when paid tier exists

Step 3 (Year 2-3):
- $1k MRR (replaces side income)
- $5k MRR (matches a job)
- $10k MRR (replaces a job)
- $30k MRR (replaces a senior job + savings)

Step 4 (Year 3+):
- Decide: scale to bigger SaaS (different game) OR coast (still write code)

### Distribution > Code

99% of SaaS fail not because of code quality. They fail because nobody knew about them.

Senior devs learn:
- SEO (writing for keywords your customers Google)
- Content marketing (blog post weekly for 2 years)
- Twitter / LinkedIn presence
- Cold email (yes, it works)
- Partnerships (integration with bigger players)

A mediocre product with great distribution beats a great product with no distribution.

## The Agency Path

If freelance, consider:
- Year 1: solo, $80-150/hr
- Year 2: hire a contractor for support
- Year 3: hire a designer + dev
- Year 5: 5-10 person agency

Revenue model:
- 50% margin on contractor work
- 100% margin on your hours
- 30% retainer base, 70% project

Pros: scale beyond your hours, can sell the business.
Cons: people management, more stress, less coding.

Most senior devs DON'T want this. Stay solo if you love coding.

## Salary Negotiation (Employee Path)

Before accepting:
- Glassdoor + Levels.fyi for market data
- Get 3 offers, leverage them
- Negotiate base + equity + bonus separately
- Ask for sign-on (one-time, no risk to them)
- Get start date that lets you decompress

Senior negotiation script:
> "I'm excited about the role. Based on my experience and similar roles at [Company X, Y], I was expecting base in the range of $180-200k. Where can we land?"

Quiet. Don't fill silence. They'll move.

Average gain from negotiating: +$15-30k/yr. 5 minutes of awkwardness = years of compound salary.

## Tech Choices for 30-Year Careers

### Bet On

- **The platform of platforms** (web, currently)
- **The language with the longest lifespan** (PHP, JS, SQL, Python)
- **The framework with momentum** (Laravel, React)
- **The boring infra** (Postgres, Redis, S3)

These have decade-plus history. They'll be here in 2040.

### Don't Bet On

- New languages that haven't survived a hype cycle
- Niche frameworks with one maintainer
- Cloud-vendor-specific tools (you'll lose them in next switch)
- Architecture you can't explain to a junior

### The AI Question (2026)

AI is changing how code is written, not whether code is written. Senior devs adapt by:
- Using AI as a pair programmer (not replacement)
- Reviewing AI output critically
- Specializing in business context AI can't replicate
- Building features that have business value, not just clever code

The dev who knows the business + uses AI well = highest paid in 2030.

## Physical / Mental Sustainability

### The 30-Year Body

You sit 8+ hours/day. Compounds:
- Standing desk (alternate sitting/standing every 30 min)
- Walk 10k steps/day
- Lift 2-3×/week (preserves muscle past 40)
- Eyes: 20-20-20 rule (every 20 min, look 20 ft away for 20 sec)
- Sleep 7-9 hrs (non-negotiable)

A senior dev with a broken back at 45 retires forced. You don't want that.

### The 30-Year Mind

- Meditation 10 min/day (compounds focus)
- Journaling (compound clarity)
- Reading non-tech books (compound wisdom)
- One screen-free day per week (compound rest)
- One real vacation per year (compound life)

Burnout at 35 is the dev career death sentence. It's preventable.

### The Hours Trap

Junior: "I'll work 80 hours/week to prove myself."
Mid: "I work 50 hours/week and burn out."
Senior: "I work 30-40 deep hours/week and out-produce 80-hour juniors."

Deep work > long work. Always.

Set hours. Honor them. Say no to "quick favors" after hours.

## The "When I Retire" Math

Retirement = passive income > expenses.

Aggressive math (Lean FIRE):
- Expenses: $40k/yr
- 4% rule: need $1M invested
- At $5k/mo saved (achievable for senior): 13 years

Comfortable math (FIRE):
- Expenses: $80k/yr
- Need $2M invested
- At $7k/mo saved: 19 years

Most senior devs reach FI in 15-20 years if they:
- Earn $120-200k
- Save 30-50% of after-tax
- Invest in index funds (boring works)
- Avoid lifestyle inflation
- Don't get divorced (financially)

This is achievable. Plan accordingly.

## The Anti-Burnout Discipline

Burnout = chronic stress without recovery. Symptoms:
- Dread Monday morning
- Code feels meaningless
- Snap at family
- Insomnia
- Constant fatigue
- Cynicism about everything

Treatment:
1. Take 2 weeks off. Real off. No Slack, no email.
2. Talk to a therapist.
3. Re-evaluate the job/client. Switch if needed.
4. Set boundaries (no Slack after 6pm).
5. Reconnect with non-tech identity (family, hobbies, friends).

Prevention:
- Take vacations even when "busy"
- Quit jobs that feel toxic
- Refuse projects that don't pay enough
- Have hobbies unrelated to tech

A burned-out senior is unhireable. Protect the asset.

## The Network Compound

By year 10, your network is worth more than your skills.

How to build it:
- Be useful to 10 people for free (early career)
- Stay in touch with old coworkers (2 messages/year)
- Show up to local meetups (yes, in person)
- Speak at conferences (even small ones)
- Open-source what you can
- Write a blog (even if 10 readers)

In year 15, your network sends you all your work.
In year 20, your network is your insurance policy.

## What to Say No To

The longer your career, the more you say no:

- ❌ Projects below your rate
- ❌ Clients who can't articulate the problem
- ❌ Jobs that lie about hours
- ❌ Stack you don't believe in
- ❌ Bosses who micromanage
- ❌ Equity-only compensation
- ❌ "We're like family" workplaces (red flag)
- ❌ Anything that requires unlearning your discipline

Saying no to bad work = capacity for good work.

Have:

## The Daily Habits That Compound

### Morning
- Walk 10 min before coding (wakes brain)
- Glass of water before coffee
- 5 min planning the day (one big thing, not 10 things)

### Working
- 90-min focus blocks
- 10-min break between blocks
- Lunch away from screen (always)
- Stop at hard end of day (no "just one more thing")

### Evening
- Walk again
- One non-tech book chapter
- Phone away by 9pm
- Sleep 10pm-6am

### Weekly
- Sunday review (what worked, what didn't)
- Sunday planning (one big thing for the week)
- One full day off-screen

### Monthly
- Personal retro (career, money, health)
- Update resume / portfolio (even when not job hunting)
- Skill audit (what's new I should learn?)

### Yearly
- 1-2 weeks REAL vacation
- Annual review of finances
- Career check: am I still growing?
- Goals for next year (3 big ones, not 30)
